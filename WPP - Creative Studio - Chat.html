My thought process

Yes — there are a bunch of “not obvious, but really important” things that can save you money, stress, or a failed closing.

Here’s a practical checklist, grouped by timing:

Before you list

1) Clear up title and permit issues early

These are easy to miss and can delay closing:

  • unpaid liens or judgments
  • old contractor liens
  • open building permits
  • unpermitted finished basements, decks, garages, etc.
  • boundary/fence encroachments
  • easements or HOA violations

If you know about any of these, address them before you go under contract.

2) Gather documents buyers may ask for

Not intuitive, but very useful:

  • utility bills
  • property tax bill
  • HOA rules/fees and recent meeting notices
  • warranties for roof, HVAC, appliances, water heater, etc.
  • receipts for repairs and improvements
  • permits and inspection records
  • septic/well records if applicable
  • survey, if you have one

3) Make a list of what stays and what goes

A lot of closing disputes happen over “fixtures”:

  • curtain rods vs curtains
  • mounted TVs and brackets
  • wall shelves
  • mirrors
  • light fixtures
  • sheds
  • ring cameras / smart doorbells
  • appliances
  • satellite dishes
  • garage shelving

If you plan to remove something that is attached, clarify it in writing with your agent and buyer.

4) Deal with smart-home tech now

This is overlooked constantly:

  • disconnect your cameras, doorbell, thermostat, alarm app, garage opener, and smart locks from your accounts
  • factory reset devices you’re leaving
  • change codes/guest access
  • remove stored Wi‑Fi networks and personal data from TVs, speakers, fridges, etc.

5) Consider a pre-listing inspection

Not required, but it can reveal issues before a buyer does:

  • roof
  • HVAC
  • plumbing leaks
  • electrical problems
  • foundation/moisture issues

Even if you don’t fix everything, knowing ahead of time helps you price and disclose properly.

While the home is listed

6) Control the “showing experience”

Small things matter a lot:

  • remove medication, mail, passports, tax forms, and anything with personal info
  • store valuables, jewelry, firearms, and spare keys
  • clean closets, pantry, under sinks, garage, attic, and crawl spaces
  • neutralize odors from pets, smoke, cooking, or litter boxes
  • secure pets before showings
  • leave lights on and open curtains for photos/showings
  • keep the temperature comfortable

7) Be careful with over-disclosing online

Don’t mention things publicly that you would only disclose through the proper legal forms. Let your agent guide what goes in the listing and what belongs in disclosures.

8) Keep your utility services on

Even when you’re not living there normally, buyers, inspectors, and appraisers may need:

  • electricity
  • water
  • gas
  • internet if smart systems depend on it

Also, don’t shut off services too early after going under contract.

After you get an offer

9) Verify the buyer’s financing strength

Not all “pre-approved” buyers are equally solid. What matters:

  • lender reputation
  • down payment amount
  • cash vs financed
  • contingency clauses
  • closing timeline

A strong offer with clean terms can be better than a slightly higher but shaky one.

10) Watch the contingencies closely

Common ones:

  • inspection contingency
  • appraisal contingency
  • financing contingency
  • sale-of-current-home contingency

The number and length of contingencies often matter as much as the price.

11) Don’t assume “under contract” means done

Deals can still fall apart from:

  • appraisal issues
  • inspection renegotiation
  • title problems
  • buyer financing failure
  • missed deadlines

Stay responsive and keep your paperwork organized.

During inspection/appraisal

12) Don’t make the house look worse than it is

This sounds obvious, but buyers and appraisers react to:

  • burnt-out bulbs
  • tripped breakers
  • dripping faucets
  • dirty filters
  • dead smoke detector batteries
  • cluttered access to attic/crawlspace/electrical panel

Fix the cheap stuff first.

13) Make sure everything important is accessible

Buyers often need access to:

  • attic
  • HVAC
  • furnace
  • water heater
  • crawl space
  • electrical panel
  • septic tank area
  • well equipment
  • pool equipment

Blocked access can create delays or bad inspection notes.

As closing approaches

14) Get a payoff statement from your lender

This is very important and easy to underestimate. Your mortgage payoff is not just the balance on your last statement. You’ll want:

  • official payoff quote
  • any per diem interest
  • HOA payoff, if applicable
  • property tax proration details

15) Confirm who pays closing costs and transfer taxes

These vary by state, county, and contract. Don’t assume the net proceeds you see in your head are accurate. Ask for a seller net sheet early.

16) Check for automatic payments tied to the house

People forget:

  • homeowners insurance
  • mortgage
  • HOA dues
  • utility autopay
  • alarm monitoring
  • pest control
  • irrigation service
  • trash pickup

Cancel or transfer those at the right time.

17) Plan for prorations

You may owe or receive prorated amounts for:

  • property taxes
  • HOA dues
  • prepaid insurance
  • fuel oil/propane
  • rent-back or occupancy periods

On closing day and after

18) Don’t move out too early if possession is later than closing

Sometimes “closing” and “possession” are different. Make sure you know:

  • when funds are actually disbursed
  • when the buyer gets keys
  • whether there’s a rent-back
  • whether you can stay past closing, and under what terms

19) Leave the right things behind

Typically helpful to leave:

  • all keys, fobs, remotes
  • garage door openers
  • mailbox key
  • gate codes
  • appliance manuals
  • warranty docs
  • paint colors/brands
  • pool/sprinkler instructions
  • security/alarm contacts, if relevant

20) Take photos before you hand it over

This is a smart, non-obvious step:

  • rooms in move-out condition
  • serial numbers of major appliances if relevant
  • condition of items left behind
  • final meter readings

Good for your records in case of disputes.

21) Forward mail and update your address everywhere

Don’t forget:

  • USPS mail forwarding
  • banks
  • employer
  • IRS/state tax agency
  • insurance companies
  • DMV
  • voter registration
  • subscriptions
  • doctors and schools

22) Keep documents for tax time

Save:

  • closing disclosure
  • settlement statement
  • receipts for major improvements
  • records of selling expenses
  • any documents related to capital improvements

These can matter for taxes, especially if there’s a gain.

A few especially easy-to-miss “gotchas”

  • Smoke and CO detectors: make sure they work before listing and through closing.
  • Pest issues: termite/pest problems can derail negotiations.
  • Water damage/mold: even minor signs can scare buyers.
  • Septic/well systems: buyers often want maintenance records and may require inspections.
  • HOA transfer rules: some HOAs require fees, questionnaires, or approval forms.
  • Security/privacy: remove family photos, prescriptions, school paperwork, and anything with account numbers.
  • Open permits: these can stop closing in some areas.
  • Solar panels: if leased or financed, this can get complicated; disclose early.
  • Car chargers / smart home subscriptions: some are tied to accounts and need transfer/reset.

My thought process

Great move — a copywriter portfolio is actually one of the easiest sites to build yourself. It's mostly text, a few samples, and contact info. You don't need anything fancy.

The honest big picture first

You'll still pay for a domain name (~$10-15/year). That's basically unavoidable. But hosting can be completely free, so you'd go from ~$200+/year to ~$12/year.

What I'd actually recommend for you

Start with plain HTML & CSS

For a portfolio site, it's genuinely the right tool:

  • No monthly fees, no platform lock-in
  • Fast loading
  • Full control over how it looks and reads
  • Not that hard to learn for a simple site
  • Will teach you skills that carry over to everything else

You do not need JavaScript, React, WordPress, or any fancy framework for this.

Where to host it for free

These are legitimate, reliable options:

HostCostNotes
GitHub PagesFreeMost popular for simple sites
NetlifyFree tierVery easy to use, great for beginners
Cloudflare PagesFreeFast, reliable

GitHub Pages is probably the best starting point. You push your files, and it becomes a live website.

Where to buy your domain

  • Namecheap — cheap, reliable, good interface
  • Cloudflare Registrar — sells at cost, no markup
  • Porkbun — also cheap and straightforward
  • Avoid GoDaddy (pushy upsells) and buying through your host (hard to move later)

What your site actually needs (keep it simple)

For a copywriter portfolio, you really only need a few pages:

  1. Home — short, punchy headline. What you do, who for, what makes you different. You're a copywriter — this is your chance to shine.
  2. Work/Portfolio — 5-8 samples. Categorized if helpful (ads, web, email, etc.)
  3. About — brief, personality-forward
  4. Contact — email, LinkedIn, a form if you want one

That's it. Resist the urge to add more.

How to learn HTML/CSS quickly

You don't need to be a developer. You need maybe 20-30 hours to get comfortable enough for a basic portfolio.

Best free resources:

  • freeCodeCamp.org — structured, beginner-friendly
  • The Odin Project — more thorough if you want depth
  • Kevin Powell on YouTube — best CSS teacher online, hands down
  • MDN Web Docs — your reference bible once you're building

Paid but worth it if you want faster:

  • Scrimba (~$20/month, interactive coding in the browser)

Practical workflow once you know the basics

1. Write your code in VS Code (free editor)
2. View it in your browser locally
3. Push it to GitHub (free)
4. Connect GitHub to Netlify or use GitHub Pages
5. Point your domain to your host
6. Live website

A few things specific to a copywriting portfolio

  • Typography matters more than design — pick one or two clean fonts (Google Fonts is free) and make the reading experience excellent
  • Load speed matters — keep images small, don't add bloat
  • Show the work, not just a link to it — if you can, show the actual copy on the page with context (what was the brief, what was the result)
  • Your homepage headline is a sample of your work — treat it that way
  • Don't use a template that looks like everyone else's portfolio

If coding feels like too much right now

If you try it and genuinely hate it, the next cheapest option is:

  • Carrd.co — ~$19/year for a custom domain, surprisingly capable for portfolios
  • Framer — free tier available, very design-forward
  • Cargo — popular with creatives, ~$13/month but beautiful

But honestly, for a copywriter portfolio, plain HTML/CSS is totally learnable and worth it. Want a suggested learning path or a rough starter code structure?
